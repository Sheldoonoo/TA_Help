Starting the SDT Experimental Wizard

 

The SDT Experimental Wizard will guide you through the set up of different types of Simultaneous DSC-TGA (SDT) experiments.

Description

The SDT is an instrument capable of making two different thermal analysis measurements simultaneously on the same sample.  Depending on which operational mode is used, these measurements are heat flow (DSC) and weight change (TGA) or differential thermal analysis (DTA) and weight change.  Simultaneous measurement of two properties not only improves productivity but also simplifies interpretation of results.  The information provided allows differentiation between thermal events that have no associated weight change (e.g., melting, phase change) and those which involve a weight change (e.g., decomposition).

To Start

Click the  button to start the Experimental Wizard. The opening window is displayed listing the following types of experiments. Select the desired type of SDT experiment to perform from those listed on the window.

For more information on a type of test click on the link.

  • SDT Temperature Ramp experiments are performed while heating the material at a constant rate from ambient to a temperature above the point of total decomposition or to the upper temperature limit of the instrument (1500Â°C).

  • SDT Cyclic experiments heat and cool the sample for multiple cycles to provide information about the reversibility of transitions and the long-term thermal stability of the material.

  • SDT Isothermal experiments are used to assess the thermal stability of materials over time at elevated temperatures.  They can also be used to assess the rate of volatiles evolution/drying at a specific temperature, as well as adsorption/desorption of gases in applications such as catalyst reactivity, porosity of ceramics, or oxidative stability.

  • SDT Stepwise Isothermal experiments use heating and isothermal holds, which often improve weight change resolution because weight changes are time dependent as well as temperature dependent.  Hence, this approach gives a lower temperature weight change more time to complete, thereby reducing overlap with the onset of subsequent higher temperature weight changes.

To Proceed:

Click the radio button for the type of experiment desired, then select Next to proceed or select Back to return to the previous window.
