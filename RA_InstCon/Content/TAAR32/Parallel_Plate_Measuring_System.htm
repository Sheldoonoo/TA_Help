Parallel Plate Measuring System

 

The parallel plate system allows samples containing particles to be effectively measured. You can set the gap to any distance, thereby eliminating the problems due to particles size. (See What Diameter and Gap Do I Use? below.) A good rule of thumb for particulate materials is to set a gap size set at least 10 times greater than the largest particle size. For example, if the maximum particle size is 100 mm, you should set the gap to at 1000 mm. The main disadvantage of a parallel plate system is that the stress is not uniform across the entire diameter. However, the software compensates for this fact. The shear stress and shear rate factors given are with respect to the rim.

Stress and shear rate factors for a parallel plate can be used to determine the maximum stresses and shear rates available with a particular diameter and gap.

What Diameter and Gap Do I Use?



As a general rule the lower the viscosity of the sample the larger the diameter of the plate required. This is because the applied torque is applied over a larger surface area and so lower stresses can be applied.

Note that the smaller the gap the higher the maximum shear rate obtainable. If you wish to measure at lower shear rate values, a larger gap is required.

Although any gap can theoretically be used with a parallel plate, considerations such as temperature gradients and plate flatness usually constrain you to a range of 500 to 2000 microns. The minimum gap for a particular plate can be determined by measuring a standard oil and plotting viscosity vs. gap.
