MDSC Procedure for Measuring Thermal Conductivity

This section contains the recommended procedure for the measurement of thermal conductivity by modulated DSC.  Whereas these conditions may not be ideally unique for the measurement, these are the guidelines by which the experiments have been performed, and satisfactory results have been generated.

Setting Up the Experiment

Before you begin the experiment, you must set up the conditions that will be used to measure the thermal conductivity.

  • Modulation Conditions:  The MDSC modulation conditions must be chosen so as to optimize the heat capacity measurement and minimize thermal lag during the experiment.  Recommended modulation conditions are a period of 80 sec, and a modulation amplitude of ±0.5°C.  A heat capacity calibration should be performed under these conditions prior to the start of the experiment.
  • Temperature of Measurement:  Thermal conductivity calibration using the polystyrene calibrant is performed prior to the measurement.  The thermal conductivity of the polystyrene has been well documented in the region between ambient temperature and 60°C, therefore calibration and subsequent measurement in this temperature range is recommended.

Calibration and Measurement Procedure

The following sections contain a suggested procedure for calibration and measurement of thermal conductivity at a temperature in the recommended operating range.

Calibrating the Instrument

  1. Weigh the thin polystyrene disk, record the weight and encapsulate the disk in a standard aluminum pan.  

NOTE:  Some disks may be slightly larger than the DSC pan, making them difficult to encapsulate.  The diameter of the disks may be reduced by polishing the edges with 600 grit emery paper to reduce their size.

Select the modulated mode on the DSC, and make sure the heat capacity signal is saved.  Create the following method:

1)  Data Storage OFF

2)  Equilibrate at 30°C

3)  Modulate ±0.5°C every 80 seconds

4)  Data Storage ON

5)  Isothermal for 20 minutes

Using an empty aluminum pan on the reference side, measure the heat capacity of the thin polystyrene sample with the above method. If calibration at additional temperatures is desired, the method can be repeated by substituting the additional temperatures of interest into segment 2).

  1. When the method is finished, plot the Heat Capacity signal versus Time. The signal should fluctuate at the beginning, but eventually flatten out.  Mark the curve value at the flattest part of the line, and label with “signal value” in the units J/g °C.  This is the Specific Heat Capacity of the polystyrene at the measurement temperature.
  2. Weigh the thick polystyrene test specimen. Record the weight, and using a set of calipers, record the diameter and height of the specimen. Place a small drop of the silicone oil on the sample and reference sides of the DSC cell. Place a thin aluminum disk over each drop of oil. Carefully position the thick sample (which has been wetted with oil on the bottom side) on the aluminum disk covering the sample side of the DSC cell.  Input the weight of the sample on the Experimental Parameters screen, and run the method listed in (1).
  3. When the method is finished, plot the Heat Capacity signal versus Time. The signal should fluctuate at the beginning, but eventually flatten out. Mark the curve value at the flattest part of the line, and label with “signal value” in the units mJ/°C. This is the Apparent Heat Capacity of the polystyrene at the measurement temperature.  
  4. Calculate the experimental thermal conductivity of the polystyrene sample using the following equation:

Ko = (8LC2)/(CpMd2P)

where: L  =  sample length (mm)

C  =  apparent heat capacity (mJ/°C)

Cp =  specific heat capacity (mJ/mg·°C; same as J/g·°C)

M  =  thick specimen mass (mg)

d  =  thick specimen diameter (mm)

P  =  period of measurement (sec)

This calculated value is the uncorrected thermal conductivity of polystyrene, and is used to generate the calibration constant. The calibration constant corrects for heat loss through the sides of the specimen.

  1. Calculate the calibration constant using the following equation:

D  =  (Ko· Kr)0.5 - Kr

where:  Ko = uncorrected thermal conductivity (calculated in  step 5)

and       Kr = reference thermal conductivity

The calibration constant should have a value of ca. 0.01 to 0.05 W/°C·m.

The calibration constant can vary slightly as a function of temperature. It is recommended therefore, to calculate a calibration constant for each unique temperature of measurement.

 

Determining Accuracy

Steps 7 and 8 are used to determine the accuracy of the technique.

  1. The accuracy of the technique can be measured by repeating steps 1 through 5 using the reference material, PMMA.  After the uncorrected thermal conductivity is calculated in step 5, substitute this value (Ko) and the calibration constant (D) (step 6) into the following equation to calculate the corrected value of thermal conductivity for PMMA:

K = [Ko - 2D + (Ko2-4DKo)0.5]/2

NOTE: Do not recalculate the calibration constant for each material analyzed. Calculate it once using polystyrene, and apply this value to all subsequent measurements.

  1. Compare the calculated thermal conductivity of PMMA with the literature value. The measurement should be within 5% of the reference value. Minor errors can arise from experimental technique. Major errors generally indicate incorrect calculations.

Measuring Thermal Conductivity on Samples

After the first 8 steps have been completed, follow the directions in the step below to conduct experiments on various samples.

  1. Subsequent thermal conductivity measurements on materials of interest can be performed by repeating steps 1 through 5 to generate the uncorrected thermal conductivity, then using the equation in step 7, a corrected value of thermal conductivity is calculated.

The calibration constant is a function of temperature and sample dimension.  To maximize the accuracy and precision of subsequent measurements, the sample specimen must be formed as identical to the calibrant specimen as possible.  In addition, if measurements are taken at varying temperatures, unique calibration constants for each temperature should be determined to maximize accuracy.  It is recommended that measurements be taken at the temperatures documented on the reference material literature.
