Transient Testing

In a Creep test, a constant force (stress) is applied to the sample and the resulting displacement (strain) is measured as a function of time.

The creep compliance [J(t)] is calculated by dividing the time-dependent strain g(t) by the applied stress t.  Once the creep compliance has reached a steady state, the sample recovery can be measured by instantaneously removing the stress (stress = 0), and monitoring the recovered strain as a function of time.

The recoverable compliance can be calculated by dividing the time-dependent recoverable strain gr(t) by the stress applied in the creep zone.  The recoverable compliance will eventually stabilize to a constant value.  If the response is linear, the stabilized value of compliance is referred to as the equilibrium recoverable compliance, Jeo.

Viscoelastic behavior in creep can be represented by the Voigt model.  This model consists of a spring and a dashpot combined in parallel.  Upon the application of stress, the dashpot acts to exponentially retard the growth of strain with time.  When the stress is removed, the dashpot again exponentially retards the recovery of strain.  The time constant (l) is known as the retardation time and represents the characteristic time dependence of the system.

In a Stress Relaxation test, a strain is instantaneously applied to the sample, and the stress required to maintain that strain is measured as a function of time.  The stress relaxation modulus [G(t) using shear sandwich clamps or E(t) using tension, compression, or bending clamps] is calculated as the time-dependent stress divided by the constant strain.  The sample recovery can also be monitored with time upon release of the strain.

NOTE:  A negative strain value must be entered when the compression clamp is used in the stress relaxation mode.

Viscoelastic behavior in stress relaxation can be represented by the Maxwell model.  This model consists of a spring and a dashpot combined in series.  Since the two elements are in series, when the strain is applied, a step change in strain results in deformation of the spring because the dashpot cannot respond instantaneously to the step change.  Once the spring has responded, the stress of the system is at a maximum, but the gradual response of the dashpot is to extend itself, allowing the spring (and the stress) to relax.
