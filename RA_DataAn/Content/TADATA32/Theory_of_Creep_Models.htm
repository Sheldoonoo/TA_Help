Theory of the Creep Models

 

This topic describes the theory behind creep models used with the TA Instruments Rheology Advantage Data Analysis program.

In a creep experiment a constant shear stress, s, is placed on a sample, and the resulting shear strain, g(t), monitored as a function of time. It is usual to quote the results in the form of the creep compliance, J(t), defined by:

J(t) = g(t) / s   (1)

A creep test is performed at a stress which is within the regime of linear viscoelasticity, in other words where J(t) is independent of the magnitude of s, a condition which usually holds only for low stresses.

 

Formally, the stress history, s(t), is defined by:

s(t) = s0 h(t)    (2)

where h(t) represents the unit (Heaviside) step function with h = 0 for t < 0 and h = 1 for t > 0 (Figure 1) [1]

But J(t) can be considered to be the sum of three separate parts, i.e.:

J(t) = J0 + Jd(t) + Jv(t)  (3)

where J0 is the instantaneous compliance, i.e. J at t = 0, Jd(t) is the delayed compliance and Jv is a viscous contribution.

These contributions can be explained through the use of the traditional spring and dashpot models, in which an ideal elastic response is modelled by a spring of compliance, J, and an ideal viscous response by a dashpot of viscosity h. The response of the spring to a constant applied stress is to deform instantaneously, with g = Js, and to recover its original dimensions when the stress is removed. The response of a dashpot is to continue to deform at a constant rate, with shear rate = s/h.

Separating the variables and integrating, the delayed compliance can often be satisfactorily modelled by a single element Voigt model, which gives:

Jd(t) = J [1-exp (-t/l)]  (8)

where J is a single value compliance, and l is referred to as the relaxation time.
