Preparing Film/Fiber Samples (Tension Experiments)

The method used to prepare and load your sample varies according to the sample type. Two methods available are for the film/fiber accessory, see Using the Aluminum Balls, Using the Stainless Steel Clamps - TMA Q400 Probe or Using the Stainless Steel Clamps - TMA 2940 Probe for more information.

Regardless of the method chosen, use the following guidelines when preparing and loading samples on the film/fiber probe for dynamic experiments:

  • Maintain a minimum length to width ratio of at least 16:1
  • Use a dynamic force of 0.05 to 0.2N, recommend starting with 0.1N
  • Use a static force of 0.2 to 0.4N
  • Use a frequency of 0.05 to 0.5Hz, recommend starting with 0.5Hz
  • See also:  Film/Fiber Probe

Preparing Flexural Samples (Three-Point Bend Experiments)

Use the following experimental guidelines for static experiments using the flexure probe:

  • No wider than the probe being used.
  • Uniform width and thickness
  • Thick enough to be rigid when placed on flexure platform
  • Long enough so that it does not fall through the fixture under load due to vibration.  For example, use samples 7 to 10 mm long for the quartz fixture or 14 to 16 mm long for the low-friction aluminum fixture.

Use the following experimental guidelines for dynamic experiments using the flexure probe:

  • Maintain a minimum length to width ratio of at least 4:1
  • Maintain a minimum length to thickness ratio of 10:1
  • Use a dynamic force of 0.05 to 0.2N, recommend starting with 0.1N
  • Use a static force of 0.1 to 0.3N
  • Use a frequency of 0.05 to 0.5Hz, recommend starting with 0.5Hz
  • See also:  Flexure Accessory

Preparing Expansion & Macroexpansion Samples (Compression Experiments)

  • Cut samples to same diameter as probe head. Note: If the sample diameter is larger than the probe head, enter the diameter of the probe head as the sample diameter.
  • Samples should be as flat as possible, with parallel ends, to ensure stable placement on the stage.
  • For static experiments:  Samples should be long enough (5 to 10 mm for most materials) for adequate resolution, keeping in mind that large samples may experience temperature gradients during high heating rates.
  • Thermoplastic samples can be heated and formed into suitable specimens and then cooled; however, this process may change important thermal history.
  • If you plan to run samples that may melt and adhere to the stage, it is best to use the quartz protective wafers (PN 944341.901) under and over the sample.

Use the following experimental guidelines:

  • To achieve the best compression data it is highly recommended that a new oscillatory compliance calibration be performed each time the probe is installed.
  • Apply just enough static force to seat the probe on the sample. We recommend using 0.05N for foam and 0.2 to 0.3N for rubbers.
  • Apply enough dynamic force to get at least 3 Âµm of length amplitude, but not enough to crush the sample. We recommend using 0.1N for foam and 0.2 to 0.3N for rubbers.
  • Use a frequency of 0.05 to 0.5Hz, we recommend starting with 0.5Hz
  • See also:  Expansion Probe.

Preparing Penetration Samples

  • Samples should be as flat as possible, with parallel ends, to ensure stable placement on the stage.
  • Samples should be long enough (5 to 10 mm for most materials) for adequate resolution, keeping in mind that large samples may experience temperature gradients during high heating rates.
  • Thermoplastic samples can be heated and formed into suitable specimens and then cooled; however, this process may change important thermal history.
  • If you plan to run samples that may melt and adhere to the stage, it is best to use the quartz protective wafers (PN 944341.901) under and over the sample.  See the next section for information.
  • See also:  Penetration Probe.
