Choosing the TGA Q5000 IR Purge Gases

You can control the sample atmosphere during TGA experiments by connecting a purge gas to the system. Purge gas is distributed separately to two parts of the TGA: the furnace and the balance chamber.

  • Recommended TGA Purge Gases: Helium, nitrogen, oxygen, air, argon

  • To change the gas type, access the Instrument Preferences/MFC Page from the Tools menu.

  • Other gases should be used with caution. If used, they must be dry and at atmospheric pressure. Only gases compatible with instrument components that will be in contact with the purge gas can be used. The exposed instrument components are made of:  platinum, platinum-rhodium, quartz, silicon carbide, kanthal wire, and alumina; in addition to copper and aluminum (both of which are cold surfaces so their reactivity will be less). Contact TA Instruments for further details before using potentially reactive or corrosive gases.

  • The gases can be as specified above, but reactive and corrosive gases such as sulfur dioxide (SO2), ammonia (NH3), hydrogen chloride (HCL), carbon monoxide (CO), carbon dioxide (CO2), and hydrogen sulfide (H2S) may also be used. Select Purge Gas Type: "Other." While no long-term testing of reactive and corrosive gases has been performed, the use of such gas mixtures is deemed acceptable for use with the furnace under the following guidelines:

    1. The reactive or corrosive gases should not exceed a five percent concentration in one of the above acceptable purge gases.

    2. The gas mixture MUST be dry and be at atmospheric pressure.

    3. The use of such gas mixtures should not be a long-term situation.

    4. The reactive or corrosive gas mixtures selected must be chemically compatible with furnace components with which the gas mixture will be in contact. These include platinum, platinel II thermocouple, quartz, silicon carbide, stainless steel (30x), Inconel® 600 and Kanthal A1 respectively.

  • The balance purge maintains a positive pressure in the balance chamber to prevent decomposition products from contaminating the sensitive balance mechanism.

  • The purge flow through the furnace is horizontal to the sample, permitting rapid removal of decomposition products from the sample environment.

  • Purge gas can be obtained from a pressurized cylinder or an in-house supply source. Gas supplied from an in-house source should be passed through a sieve dryer to remove any trace of moisture before it enters the TGA.

  • Oxidative stability experiments use nitrogen for the primary gas and oxygen for the secondary gas.

  • You can switch between the two gases (Gas #1 and Gas #2) by using a gas switch segment in your method. See also:  Switching gases in your experiment.

NOTE: If you are using laboratory purge gas, rather than bottle purge gas, you will need to install an external drier.

WARNING:  Do not use hydrogen or any other explosive gas in the furnace  Oxygen may be used as a purge gas, but the furnace must be kept clean of volatile hydrocarbons to prevent combustion.

WARNING:  Do not use any liquid in the purge lines.

Setting the Flow Rates

The gas entries on the Notes Page of the Experiment View are used to specify the gas and the associated flow rate for each run. These settings will automatically set the gas and flow rate on a run by run basis. The scheduled run's purge settings will take effect immediately once the Apply button is clicked. (If an outside purge is selected, these entries will be for information only.)

It is important to maintain the proper ratio of flow rates between the balance chamber and the furnace housing. Having the separate balance chamber purge prevents decomposition gases from entering the balance chamber.

  • Recommended purge rate: The recommended flow distribution should be 10 mL/min to the balance chamber and 25 mL/min to the furnace.

Before you start the purge gas, make sure that the desired gas is connected to the purge ports, that all lines are clear, and that your supply of purge gas is sufficient for the experiment.

Always maintain constant purge flow rates and distribution throughout your experiment; changing the purge during an experiment can affect the data.

Use in an Low Oxygen Atmosphere

If you choose to perform TGA experiments in a low oxygen atmosphere, a few extra precautions to the purge gas system and instrument setup are necessary to ensure a low oxygen environment.

Back to top

Purge Gas System

When performing TGA experiments in a low oxygen atmosphere, take the following precautions:

  • Use a high purity, inert gas of grade 5 or better.  It may be necessary to use an oxygen trap in-line, depending on the purity grade.

  • Choose a 2-stage regulator of diaphragm construction for high purity applications.

  • Use copper or stainless steel tubing from the gas regulator.

  • Allow the TGA to prepurge (under closed conditions) for at least 30 minutes after first turning on your purge gas.  

  • Increase the standard purge rate during this time to 100 mL/minute flow into the balance chamber and 100 mL/minute into the furnace.

Back to top

Instrument Set-Up

When performing TGA experiments in a low oxygen atmosphere, take the following steps to set up your instrument:

  • Readjust the flow rate to the standard operating flow rates of 10 mL/min into the balance chamber and 25 mL/min into the furnace.

  • Use nitrogen as your air cool gas or unselect the air cool option. Use the Post Test function to disable air cool. See Entering Post Test Options.

  • Tare sample pan (as needed).

  • Load sample and close system

  • Purge 20 minutes, if possible, before starting a run.

  • When run is complete, allow the furnace to cool in the closed position until the temperature is less than 350°C.  This can be automatically programmed into your method; after last segment add an equilibrate at 350°C step.  Another option is to change method-end (post-test) conditions to leave furnace closed at method end.

  • When using air or oxygen during an experiment, introduce new gas through furnace purge port only and switch back to nitrogen before cooling down.

  • When the TGA is idle, leave the system closed and continue purging with nitrogen.

Switching Gases in Your Experiment

You can set up your method so that the purge gas is switched during the procedure. This is done using the Gas Segment, which controls the switching of the gas between Gas 1 and 2. In addition, you can also change the flow rate within a method using the flow rate method segment.

During an active experiment you can switch gases using the Control/Gas menu or the GAS button on the instrument's Control touch screen.
