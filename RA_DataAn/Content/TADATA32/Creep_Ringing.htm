Creep Ringing

 

A standard creep experiment is performed by applying a constant, low, stress, s0 to a sample of material, and monitoring the strain response, g(t). It is usual to display the data graphically either by plotting g(t) against time, t, or the creep compliance J(t) against t, where J(t) is defined as g(t)/s0. Such a plot is referred to as a "creep curve". If s0 does not exceed a sample dependent critical stress, sc, then J(t) is, for practical purposes, independent of the applied stress, behavior which can be treated by the theory of linear viscoelasticity.

This theory is discussed to some extent in all the standard text books on rheology [e.g. 1, 2], and at length in Tschoegl [3], but most texts are concerned only with J(t) as a material function, and not with the practical details of its measurement. In particular, they ignore the contribution of the measuring system inertia to the response. This is often a reasonable thing to do, since, almost by definition, a creep test is conducted at a low stress, and hence low torque, and the angular velocity of the measuring system is therefore also low. For the most part, the rate of change of angular velocity is also low, which is why the inertia can be ignored. But for first the second or so after the imposition of the stress, this is not the case. Here the inertial contribution can be significant, and failure to account for it requires that this early part of the creep curve should be discarded. This is unfortunate, since the very short timescale properties of the material can be of interest to the rheologist. Usually, these would have to be explored using some other technique, such as oscillatory rheometry. Rheologists have historically tried to reduce the problem by using a sample stiffness to the measuring system inertia ratio which is as large as possible, for example by using a measuring geometry of large radius. But it is more elegant and satisfactory to include the contribution of the measuring system inertia in the analysis of the response. This approach has been taken by several groups of workers [4, 5], most recently and fully by Baravian and Quemada [6]. TA Instruments’ software introduces an extension of previous analyses.

Linear viscoelastic theory allows us to analyze the creep compliance using a combination of elements formed from the units of a Newtonian liquid and a Hookean solid. These units are often represented diagrammatically as a dashpot and a spring respectively (Figure 1). The simplest model is then the Newtonian liquid, i.e. single dashpot, described by its viscosity, h. Then JN(t) = t / h, the subscript indicating the model. The inclusion of inertia results in a function which increases monotonically with time, with a slope that approaches that of JN(t) asymptotically.

Next in order of complexity is the Maxwell model, consisting of a spring and dashpot in series (Figure 2). For this model JM(t) = 1 / G + t / h, where G is the elastic modulus of the spring, and h is the viscosity of the dashpot. When inertia is included, two cases result, an oscillating and a non-oscillating one. The oscillating (under damped) case arises when G ³ Gcritical. For the Maxwell model Gcritical = 4h2 / a, where a = I Fs / F, with I being the measuring system inertia, and Fs and F the proportionality factors between the shear stress and torque, and shear rate and angular velocity, respectively. The non-oscillating (over damped) case arises when G < 4h2 / a.

A Voigt model consists of a spring and dashpot in parallel (Figure 3). For this model, JV(t) = (1/G)[1 – exp(-tG/h)]. Again an oscillating (under damped) case, when G ³ Gcritical and a non-oscillating (under damped) case, when G < Gcritical, arise. For the Voigt model, Gcritical = h2 / 4a.

The Jeffreys model can be recast as a Voigt element and dashpot in series (Figure 5). In terms of the previous form of the model, the parameters of this arrangement are given by: J = (1/G)[hp/(hs + hp)]2, hV = hs(hs + hp)/hp and h0 = hs + hp. In this form J is the spring compliance, i.e. the reciprocal of its elastic modulus. The oscillating condition is as for the Voigt model applied to the retardation step.

It is usual to perform a creep experiment in two steps, a "retardation" step, over which the stress is applied, followed a "relaxation" or recoil, step, over which the tress is zero. The relaxation step can also be analyzed for a Voigt model, using TA Instruments’ creep ringing software.

Creep ringing analysis permits the extremely rapid determination of the viscoelastic properties of materials, including the zero shear viscosity, providing that one of the relatively simple models described above gives an adequate fit to the data. If the inertial contribution were not considered, then it would be necessary to wait for the system to achieve steady state before any analysis could be performed. For some materials this can take many minutes or even hours. In principle, creep ringing reduces the timescale to a matter of seconds.
