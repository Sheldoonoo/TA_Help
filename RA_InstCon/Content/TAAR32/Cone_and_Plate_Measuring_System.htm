Cone and Plate Measuring System

 

This is probably the most commonly used measuring system. The main advantage is that the shear rate across the entire surface is uniform. Cone and plate geometries are generally used for single phase homogeneous samples or samples with sub-micron particles. Sample containing particulate matter are usually unsuitable for cone and plate geometries as the particles will tend to migrate to the apex of the cone and will get jammed in the truncation area. Erroneous data will result.

You will notice that the cone does not finish in a perfect point. There is always a small amount shaved off, called the truncation (or T value) . This is to prevent wear on the lower plate. The small truncation only introduces a very small error, which will not affect the results. Each cone has a slightly different amount shaved off, dependent upon the cone angle. The truncation value is engraved on the stem of each cone and is the gap that must be set when using these measuring systems.

Stress and shear rate factors for a cone and plate can be used to determine the maximum stresses and shear rates available with a particular diameter and angle of cone.

The standard diameters available are 20 mm, 40 mm and 60 mm with cone angles of 0.5° to 4° in 0.5° increments.
