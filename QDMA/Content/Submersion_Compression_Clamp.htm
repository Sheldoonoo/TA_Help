Submersion Compression Clamp

 

Compression is used to measure the properties of low to medium modulus materials, including gels and weak elastomers. These clamps are used to test low to medium modulus samples while they are submerged in a fluid. The clamp is primarily designed to evaluate polymeric materials in aqueous or buffer solutions at temperatures up to 80°C. Because of the volume of fluid (and its associated heat capacity), these submersion measurements are usually performed under isothermal conditions. The sample must support a preload force, so the samples should not flow. The sample should have as high a thickness-to-diameter ratio as is possible, depending on the sample preparation and instrument limits. Samples that are in the stiffness range of 100 N/m to 10,000,000 N/m can be tested.

 

WARNING:  Proper clamp selection within the instrument control software (e.g., submersion film/fiber and submersion compression) is required to ensure that the 80°C upper temperature range cannot be exceeded.

WARNING:  Flammable or volatile fluids should not be used as the submersion fluid.

CAUTION: The Submersion Compression clamp is designed primarily for isothermal experiments in the temperature range of ambient to 80°C. Heating ramp experiments can also be performed at <2°C/minute. The clamp is designed for aqueous or buffer solutions and should never be used with fluids whose vapor pressure results in volatility at the temperature of usage.

Operating Range of the Submersion Compression Clamp

Geometry Factor Equation

The geometry factor, GF, in the equation below can help you determine the optimum sample size for the sample material or determine if a sample of a particular size can be measured.  

GF (1/mm) = Fe(T/A)

where:

T = Sample Thickness (height) in mm

A = Sample Cross Sectional Area in mm2

Fe = Sample Shear Factor (non-dimensional).

The sample modulus is calculated by multiplying the measured sample stiffness, Ks, by the geometry factor, GF.  

Modulus (MPa) = (Ks x GF)/1000

Modulus (MPa) = [Ks x Fe(L/A)]/1000

where the measurable stiffness range of the instrument is a fixed quantity between 100 to 10,000,000 N/m.  

Note we can solve the modulus equation above for stiffness as follows

Ks (N/m) = 1000 x (Modulus/GF) or

Ks (N/m) = 1000 x [(Modulus x A)/Fe x L]

Since the modulus of the material is an intrinsic property—i.e., independent of the sample dimensions—it can be seen that the sample dimensions must be selected to yield stiffness values within the allowable measurable range of the instrument.

Mounting Samples

After the submersion compression clamp has been installed and calibrated on the DMA, you can mount the sample using the following steps:

  1. Press the CLAMP key to raise the clamp, then press the DRIVE Float/Lock key to lock the clamp in the raised position.

  2. Loosen the hex screws holding the top bar and compression disk (moveable clamp) onto the yoke and remove the moveable clamp assembly.

  3. Place the sample in the bottom of the tank, making sure that it does not extend beyond the edges of the target area in the bottom as seen in the figure to the right.

  4. Replace the moveable clamp assembly and tighten the hex screws onto the yoke.

  5. Enter the preload force using the Instrument Control program. The recommended values for preload force values are 0.01 to 1 N, depending on how soft the sample is.

  6. Move the clamp all the way down using the CLAMP key.

  7. Carefully fill the fluid container (tank) to the fill line marked on the inside of the cup. It is recommended that the fluid be loaded into tank using a squeeze bottle, small funnel, pipette, or other device that will direct flow of fluid directly into tank without spilling or splashing the fluid. See the figure below.

  8. Turn on the supply line to air cool if you are not using the Gas Cooling Accessory (GCA).

CAUTION:  OVERFILLING THE CUP OR SPILLING DURING FILLING can result in fluid getting down into the air bearing, which could cause permanent damage to the instrument.

You are now ready to run an experiment as directed in the next section.

Running an Experiment

When you use the submersion compression clamp for experiments, you will need to set up the experimental parameters using the instrument control software.

Follow these general guidelines when using the submersion compression clamp:

  1. Use the Instrument Control program to select the clamp type, mode, and experimental parameters.

  2. Set up the instrument parameters.  Note that the amplitude range of the DMA is 0.5 to 10,000 microns. The upper amplitude range is limited to 100 microns when using a maximum sample thickness of 5 mm with the submersion compression clamp. This amplitude range is a physical limit that is limited by distance between the bottom of the top plate and the top edge of the cup.  However, because of the possibility of splashing during oscillations when making measurements in the submersion compression clamp, amplitudes should not exceed 100 microns regardless of the sample thickness. This clamp is a tensioning clamp; therefore, preload force and force track values must be selected. The recommended values for preload force are 0.01 to 1 N. (The preload force) may be the same value that you entered while mounting the sample, if desired.) The recommended values for force track are 120 to 150%.  

NOTE:  Temperature ramp experiments may be performed using the submersion film/fiber clamps, but only at low rates (2.0 °C/min or lower). For better thermal equilibration it may be best to perform temperature sweeps (measurements at different isothermal temperatures).

  1. Press the CLAMP key on the instrument to move the clamp faces together and compress the sample using the preload force programmed in step 1.  

  2. Press the MEASURE key.  This will apply the preload force, measure the sample thickness and automatically enter the thickness into the experimental parameters. Then the motor will start in the programmed operation mode, but the readings will not be saved to a file.

  3. View the signals. When running a dynamic experiment (multifrequency and multistrain mode) pay special attention to the following signals:

    • Amplitude:  This signal should achieve and maintain the value programmed.  If you are running a multistrain experiment, the amplitude will cycle through the values programmed.

    • Stiffness:  The stiffness should be within the  instrument’s measurable range of 100 N/m to 10,000,000 N/m.  If it is not, refer “Operating Range of Submersion Compression Clamp” to make the appropriate adjustments.

    • Drive Force:  The drive force should between 0.0001 N and 18 N.  If the drive force is not within this range, either increase the programmed amplitude to increase the drive force, or increase the stiffness of the sample by changing the physical dimensions of the sample. To increase the sample stiffness, increase the diameter and decrease the thickness of the sample.

    • Preload Force:  If force track is used, the preload force will automatically be adjusted to remain a set percentage greater than the force required to drive the sample at the programmed amplitude.  If force track is not used, the preload force should read the value set in the instrument parameters.  For more information regarding preload force, see Understanding the DMA Forces.

If the initial conditions are acceptable (i.e., smooth oscillation and good modulus values), start the experiment. See Starting a Run and/or Sequence.

If the values are not acceptable, then change the conditions appropriately.

Removing Samples

After you have finished your experiment, you can remove the sample from the submersion compression clamp as follows:

  1. Press the DRIVE Float/Lock key to lock the drive shaft.

  2. Using a pipette or syringe to avoid spilling fluid on the instrument, remove the remaining fluid from fluid tank and wipe the inside of the tank dry.

  3. Use a hex wrench to loosen the screws in the top bar.

  4. Carefully remove the top bar with the compression disk and wipe excess fluid from the compression disk.  (It is important to wipe the fluid from the compression disk so that it will not interfere with subsequent tests.)

  5. Remove the sample.

  6. Load a new sample, if desired.
