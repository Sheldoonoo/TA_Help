Introduction to Flow Tests

 

The cook, the painter, the heart surgeon and the drilling fluid engineer are all concerned with the flow properties of materials, and therefore, whether they know it or not, with the science of rheology. The word itself was not coined until the 1920’s, but the flow of liquids has been the subject of study for millennia. This study was first formalised by Isaac Newton, in the late seventeenth century, who observed that some liquids could be made to flow more easily than others, and that the flow rate of each material depended on the impetus to which they were subjected.

 

Newton lacked the vocabulary of modern materials science, and his discussion is not easy to follow, but the concepts involved are now described by rigorously defined terms, in particular stress (usually given the symbol s) and strain (g). Although these are commonly used interchangeably, in materials science they have distinct and precise meanings. Stress is the force, acting over an area, applied to a sample; strain is the amount by which it moves in response to the stress. The viscosity (h) of a liquid is then defined as the stress divided by the rate of change of strain (dg/dt or ). Newton’s original postulate can then be phrased as that the viscosity of a material is constant: the stress is proportional to the flow rate. The more easily a liquid flows, the lower the viscosity.

 

The behaviour of some materials, such as low molecular weight liquids, complies with Newton’s postulate, and can easily be predicted from a few simple experiments. But as the cook, the painter, and the others are aware, many materials show behaviour patterns which are highly complex. To predict how such materials will respond to a particular process is not always straightforward. Most of the traditional test methods used to measure viscosity (falling bars or balls, rolling balls, U tubes, efflux cups, simple rotational viscometers, and so on) can be used to predict the behaviour of materials during some processes, but not others. But modern rheometers are designed to be extremely flexible in their operation, capable of covering many processes and many types of behaviour.

 

The commonest form of non-Newtonian behaviour is shear-thinning, or pseudoplasticity, when the viscosity is not constant but decreases with increasing stress. Many industrial particulate dispersions, and polymer solutions and melts, are shear-thinning, which can be an extremely useful property. Typical are non-drip paints, which need to have a high viscosity under low stress, to prevent sedimentation, slumping or dripping, but a low viscosity at high stress for ease of application. Similar considerations apply to many printing inks, drilling fluids, cosmetics, pharmaceutical suspensions, foodstuffs, polymers and so on. A face cream must be relatively stiff and creamy to the touch, but spread easily on the skin. Drilling fluids need to have a high viscosity on standing, to hold suspended particles, but a low viscosity during pumping.

 

The reasons for shear thinning are often complex, and vary between materials. In polymer gels, pectin and gelatin for example, it occurs because of the rupture of junction zones of attraction between adjacent polymer molecules. In flocculated colloidal dispersions such as yoghurts, the flocs are broken down into smaller units, and eventually into primary particles. In deflocculated, stable, dispersions, shearing will align the particles into two dimensional arrays, facilitating flow. A few materials will shear-thicken, usually high solids dispersions; this effect is almost invariably undesirable.

 

Not only can the viscosity of materials depend on the magnitude of the applied stress, it may also depend on the length of time for which the stress is applied. The viscosity of most dispersions will decrease with time after the imposition of a stress, and will take time to recover after its removal; this effect is known as thixotropy. Like pseudoplasticity, a reasonable degree of thixotropy is often useful, for example it enables surface coatings to flow out after application, removing irregularities, before they stiffen. A third variable which can alter the viscosity of materials is temperature; the viscosity of most materials decreases with increasing temperature.

 

The complete viscometric characterisation of a liquid would therefore require shear rate to be monitored as a function of shear stress, time and temperature, but in practice such completeness is seldom necessary. It is usually enough to identify the conditions and parameters of interest and make the corresponding measurements.

 

A huge body of viscosity data has now been published for many materials, and it is remarkable that the curves of viscosity, plotted against shear rate, of almost all of these are very similar in shape. The main features of such curves are most easily seen if the data are plotted on logarithmic axes (i.e. with 0.1, 1, 10, 100 etc. equally spaced). At low shear rates a Newtonian region exists, followed at higher rates by shear thinning region which often takes the form of a power law (straight line on logarithmic axes). In some cases the complete curve is not accessible from a single experiment, and it is easy to miss this point.
