Using the DMA for Transition Temperature

DMA is a very popular and powerful technique for detecting thermo-mechanical transitions/relaxations in materials.  In terms of mechanical behavior, transitions occur in amorphous of semi-crystalline polymeric materials when the energy thresholds for certain molecular motions are exceeded.  This increased molecular mobility, caused by the increase in temperature, leads to the overall "softening" of the material (provided the material does not undergo a chemical change).  This process usually manifests itself as drop in the storage modulus (E’ or G’), a peak in the loss modulus (E” or G”), and a peak in tan d.  Other transitions due to small-scale motions, local mode relaxations, crankshaft and kink motions, rotations of terminal groups, and other side chain motions can also be detected as peaks in the loss modulus and tan d.  These so-called secondary transitions cannot be detected by a Differential Scanning Calorimeter (DSC).  The figure below shows the glass transition of a PET film.

 

The DMA clamps and the sample contribute to the total mass to the system and cause temperature lags between the furnace temperature and the sample temperature.  The extent of these lags can be determined and compensated for depending on the temperature profile employed. Temperature profiles, or methods, in the DMA can programmed in two ways: (1) step-and-hold test, or (2) temperature ramp test. See also:  Testing Considerations.

Step-and-Hold Test

In a step-and-hold test, the method should have a start temperature, an end temperature, and a temperature step size.  The sample should be held isothermally at each temperature step for a defined length of time, prior to conducting the measurement (3 to 5 min for films).  

The step-and-hold test has several advantages, including elimination of temperature lag between the furnace and sample, and the ability to use a wider selection of frequencies while the sample is isothermal.  Frequency sweeps during a temperature ramp is not recommended because of incoherence in data due to the changing of temperature during a recurring frequency sweep.  This is because the time taken to get each data point for low frequencies is large.  Therefore, the points collected may be too far apart on the temperature axis for a ramp experiment.

The disadvantages of step-and-hold are longer measurement times as the sample size increases, requiring longer equilibration (“soak”) times, or as the number of steps across a temperature region increases (either because the region is large or because an improved resolution of events is desired).  If low frequency information is needed for a range of temperatures, step-and-hold method is recommended.  

NOTE:  Step-and-hold testing is recommended for time-temperature superposition (TTS) studies.

Temperature Ramp Test

To perform a temperature ramp test, you should set up a method with a start temperature, end temperature, and rate of temperature change (e.g. 2°C/min).  The instrument simultaneously ramps the temperature and sweeps through the frequencies.  

The advantage of this method is that testing times are shorter and data can be acquired continuously, which leads to well-defined transition peaks.  

The disadvantage of this method is that the temperature lags between the sample and furnace will cause shifts in the observed transition temperatures.

When you perform a temperature ramp test, less than five frequencies are recommended.  Those frequencies should be spaced close together and should be from the highest to the lowest (e.g., 10, 5, 1 Hz).  In addition, frequencies below 0.5 Hz are not recommended when you are ramping at the heating rates above 2°C/minute.

Testing Considerations

When conducting dynamic mechanical measurements using either temperature profile test already described, the following factors should be considered:

  • Clamping arrangement —More massive clamps will cause larger temperature lags.

  • Heating/cooling rate—In temperature ramp tests, the faster the heating/cooling rate, the larger is the thermal gradient.  Fast heating rates, especially coupled with large sample/clamps, cause erroneous transition determination.

  • Sample size—The larger the sample, the larger the thermal gradient.

  • Thermocouple position—the further the thermocouple is from the sample, the larger the thermal lag.

  • Air bearing gas used—the most common gases used are air and nitrogen.  The thermal conductivity of air is slightly higher than nitrogen. For experiments higher than 400°C, the use of nitrogen is recommended.

  • Frequency—consider the oscillation frequency when designing a test, since transitions are frequency-dependent.  Higher frequencies cause transitions to shift to higher temperatures (see the figure below).   Avoid using fast temperature  ramp (5°C/min) rates and low frequencies (0.1 Hz) in a single test.
