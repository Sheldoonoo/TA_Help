Introducing Multiwave Frequency (MWF) Testing

 

During a multiwave frequency (MWF) experiment, data is gathered at not only for a single frequency, but also for additional (higher than the fundamental) values, known as harmonic multipliers. The data for all frequency values is gathered in the same time it would take to measure just the fundamental frequency in a standard oscillatory test. These attributes of the MWF technique allow it to be used either as a means to save time when performing frequency sweeps (especially at low frequencies), or as a method of study the viscoelastic properties of a material as a function of both frequency and time/temperature (for instance, when studying a change of state such as cure or gelation.

There are two main areas where the technique is useful

  • A quality control, routine, or highly repetitive type of experiment where a rheological dynamic "fingerprint" of the material is all that is required. A measurement at low frequencies (e.g., 0.5 Hz), a medium frequency (e.g., 5 Hz) and a high frequency (e.g., 20 Hz) could give a measurement of the material’s property in its viscous, transition and elastic regions.
  • Where fast changing or curing samples are being measured. MWF obviates the need to do repeat tests at different frequencies by obtaining the maximum amount of rheological data from a single experiment.

Validation of the technique is made difficult due to the time-varying nature of the experiment. The frequencies chosen must be high compared with the time of the experiment, so that the rheological properties do not change significantly within one cycle of the fundamental.

 

The MWF technique must be used with care. Careful validation of the torques and amplitudes used and produced must be conducted initially. More information regarding the expected results must be obtained prior to an actual experiment than in a standard oscillation experiment. Used with care, however, the technique is extremely useful and time saving.

Performing a MWF Test

The software allows any integer harmonic multiplier up to the 40th to be added together with the fundamental. The ratio of the torque amplitude can be, if required, adjusted at each frequency relative to the fundamental. This is an important feature since the amplitude will decrease with increasing frequency for the same applied stress, for most materials. Inertial effects, both fluid and instrument based, will reduce the amplitude of the strain wave. This can lead to very small amplitude signals (possibly at or below the instrument resolution) at the highest harmonic multiplier. To overcome this potential problem, the torque at each harmonic multiplier is selected to be higher than the fundamental by a user definable ratio, such that the strain amplitude at the higher frequencies is of similar order to that of the fundamental.

 

To ensure that the resulting data is meaningful and valid, it is necessary to ensure that the minimum and maximum torques used are within the working range of the instrument. At any given instant, the torques at the different harmonic multipliers will add together in a mathematically complex way. The worst case is when all of the harmonic multipliers are in phase with the fundamental. It is important, therefore, to ensure that the instrument has sufficient torque to achieve the desired strain.

It is also necessary to ensure that the total strains do not add up to a value that is greater than the linear viscoelastic limit of the material under test. The best way to validate a MWF technique with a material is described below.

  1. Perform a torque sweep to establish the linear viscoelastic region (LVR) at a particular frequency, such as 1 Hz.
  2. Determine the strain at which you start to see departure from linear behavior.
  3. Divide this displacement value by the number of frequencies that you wish to include in your multiwave procedure. This should be limited to a maximum of five for good resolution of the individual waveforms.
  4. The value you derive for the strain should be within the instrument's working range. If it is not, then the number of frequencies chosen will need to be less than five. This is to ensure that, if you get waveform reinforcement, you do not exceed the sample’s LVR.
  5. Perform a controlled strain frequency sweep over the range of interest, using the strain derived in step 2.
  6. Make a note of the torque value in the data table for the lowest frequency.
  7. Choose the other frequencies (greater than the fundamental selected in the previous step) at which you wish to measure.
  8. Divide these frequencies by the fundamental frequency to determine the closest harmonic multipliers.
  9. Look up the torque values nearest to the frequencies of interest and divide by the torque at the fundamental. Enter these values as the torque ratio at each harmonic multiplier.

Notes:

  • Samples with small LVR’s will not be suitable for MWF.
  • Validation of the LVR at higher and lower frequencies is also advisable.
  • In a time or temperature sweep, the frequency of the test is your fundamental and the strain is that derived in step 2.
  • If the material changes during the experiment, so may its LVR.
  • In a frequency sweep the start and end frequency and number of points determine the fundamentals and the strain is as derived in step 2.
