Understanding the Different DMA Force Parameters

 

The DMA uses several different types of force during the running of an experiment. This topic provides a brief description of these forces for details that apply to a particular clamp or operating mode, see the help associated with that clamp or mode.

Preload Force

This force is used in conjunction with the tensioning clamps (film/fiber tension, compression, penetration, and 3-point bending).  The first and foremost function that this serves is to keep the sample taut (in case of tension clamps) and to keep the movable clamp in contact with the sample (in case of compression, penetration and 3-point bending clamps).  This force is applied as soon as the MEASURE button is pressed and enables the instrument to establish the sample length in case of tension and compression clamps.  This is the prime function of Preload Force especially for Creep and Stress-Relaxation experiments.

When running an oscillation experiment, the preload force has an additional important function for all tensioning clamps.  Without any preload, the sinusoidal deformation will begin from the sampleâ€™s null or rest position.  In case of the 3-point bending or compression clamps, when the instrument applies the positive part of the sinusoidal deformation, the sample will be deformed, but when the negative part of the sinusoidal deformation is applied, the movable clamp will leave the surface of the sample!  In case of the tension clamps, the sample will buckle, resulting in erroneous data.  So, the sample has to be pre-strained by applying a static force, and then made to oscillate about that position.  When not using Force track, the entry in the preload force field is used as the static force for pre-straining.  

Typical preload forces are in the range of 0.01 to 0.5 N, depending on the stiffness of the sample.  Once should choose a force just small enough to keep the sample taut or in contact with the movable clamp, yet not large so that the sample begins to deform or creep due to it.

Force Track

If the same static force is used, say, for an oscillation temperature ramp experiment, then as the material softens, it may start stretching.  This is undesirable because the measurements would be made on a sample that has undergone a change from its initial form.  The purpose of oscillation experiments is to apply as small a perturbation to the sample as possible without destroying its mechanical integrity.  Force Track is used to circumvent this.

The Dynamic Force is that which is required to maintain the programmed amplitude at a certain frequency.  When force track is activated, the static force is no longer constant, but is dependent on the dynamic force.  Force track is essentially, the ratio of static to dynamic force.  This ratio is always greater than unity, or 100%.  In other words, if force track is used, the static force will automatically be adjusted to be a set percentage greater than the force required to drive the sample at the programmed amplitude.  If and when the sample stiffness changes, the dynamic force also changes, e.g., when the sample softens at higher temperatures, the dynamic force required to achieve the constant amplitude decreases.  Since static force is maintained in proportion with the dynamic force, it too is decreased, thereby avoiding overstraining of sample.

The following formula is used to maintain the ratio between the preload force and dynamic force (force to drive amplitude).

Static Force = Force Track x Stiffness x Amplitude

For example, if you program a Force Track of 120% and an Amplitude of 10 microns, and 0.5 N of Dynamic Force, the measured sample stiffness is 50,000 N/m. The Static Force will be as follows:

Static Force = (1.2) x (10 x 10-6 m) x (50,000 N/m) = 0.6 N
