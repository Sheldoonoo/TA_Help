DMA Dynamic Calibration

 

Dynamic calibration is used to measure several samples of known stiffness and loss to characterize the dynamic performance of the instrument. Dynamic calibration must be done in the following situations:

  • When the DMA is moved or turned off, or

  • At least once a month.

Select Instrument/Dynamic from the DMA Calibrate menu and follow the steps provided below.

Dynamic Calibration - Step 1

Install Clamps

  1. Install the 35-mm dual cantilever clamp on the DMA. If it is already installed, remove any samples, if necessary.

  2. Close the furnace.

  3. Select the Calibrate button to begin calibration. The furnace will open when the calibration is complete (approximately five minutes).

  4. Press Next to proceed to the next step.

Dynamic Calibration - Step 2

Calibrate Compliance Sample

Compliance calibration is performed using a rigid precision steel sample with known dimensions. It is used to measure the flexibility of a sample and calibrates the instrument to that flexibility. Compliance calibration is needed when you install the dual cantilever clamp for dynamic calibration.

  1. Measure the large, precision steel compliance sample, provided in the accessory kit, with calipers to determine its thickness and width.

  2. Enter the measured width and thickness of the precision steel compliance sample in the appropriate fields and enter 35 mm as the length.

  3. Mount the large compliance sample in both the fixed and floating clamps using a torque of 8 to 10 in-lbs. (See Mounting samples on the dual cantilever clamp for instructions.)

  4. Press the FURNACE key to close the DMA furnace.

  5. Select Calibrate.  The furnace will open when the clamp mass calibration has been completed. (Approximate time is five minutes.)

  6. Click the Next button to proceed. The next step requires calibration of the 0.005-inch steel sample.

Dynamic Calibration - Step 3

Calibrate 0.005 Steel

  1. Press the DRIVE key on the instrument touch screen to lock the drive shaft. Then remove the compliance sample, used in the previous step, from the clamp.

  2. Mount the 0.005-inch (0.12-mm) steel sample carefully using a torque of 8 to 10 in-lbs. Make sure that the sample is not bent or otherwise deformed. (See Mounting samples on the dual cantilever clamp for instructions.)

  3. Press the FURNACE key to close the DMA furnace.

  4. Select Calibrate to begin calibrating this sample. The furnace will open when the clamp mass calibration has completed.

  5. Click the Next button to proceed. The next step requires calibration of the 0.010-inch (0.25-mm) steel sample.

Dynamic Calibration - Step 7

Analysis

You have completed calibrating all of the steel samples. At this point, you can decide to choose one of the following options:

  • If you are planning to run low stiffness samples frequently, you may wish to calibrate additional steel samples. Select Next to calibrate up to 4 additional steel samples and go on to the next step.

  • If you feel that calibration of the first 5 steel samples is sufficient, you can select Analyze to analyze the current calibration data. The program automatically analyzes and evaluates the results from all of the compliance samples that you have already run to determine the instrument calibration constants. This process can be viewed on the Signal Display Pane. Press Next when complete to view the Calibration Report for the results of the calibration.  

NOTE:  If this calibration is not successful, repeat the calibration using 0.015-inch and 0.025-inch steel samples.  If the calibration is still not successful, repeat the calibration again using 0.020-inch and 0.050-inch steel samples.  If the calibration fails for a third time, call TA Instruments for service.

Dynamic Calibration - Step 8

Calibrate Additional Steel Samples

  1. Press the DRIVE key on the instrument touch screen to lock the drive shaft. Then remove the mounted steel sample from the clamp.

  2. Mount a new steel sample. Make sure that the sample is not bent or otherwise deformed. (See Mounting samples on the dual cantilever clamp for instructions.)

  3. Press the FURNACE key to close the DMA furnace.

  4. Select Calibrate to begin calibrating this sample. The furnace will open when the clamp mass calibration has completed.

At this point, you can decide to choose one of the following options:

  • You may wish to calibrate additional steel samples. Select Calibrate to calibrate additional steel samples (total of four additional samples).

NOTE:  Once the limit of 4 additional samples has been reached, the Analyze button will no longer be accessible.

  • If you feel that calibration of the samples already done is sufficient, you can select Analyze to analyze the current calibration data. The program automatically analyzes and evaluates the results from all of the compliance samples that you have already run to determine the instrument calibration constants. This process can be viewed on the Signal Display Pane. Press Next when complete to view the Calibration Report for the results of the calibration.

NOTE:  If this calibration is not successful, repeat the calibration using 0.015-inch and 0.025-inch steel samples.  If the calibration is still not successful, repeat the calibration again using 0.020-inch and 0.050-inch steel samples.  If the calibration fails for a third time, call TA Instruments for service.

Technical Explanation of Dynamic Calibration

The DMA dynamic model consists of three bodies: the slide/movable clamp, the frame/fixed clamp, and the cabinet/table, with viscous and elastic coupling.  The end products of Dynamic calibration are masses, spring constants, and damping coefficients for this three body model.

The calibration is performed as follows:

  1. Follow the instructions in the software to collect damping and stiffness data at a wide range of frequencies, for a number of spring steel samples that have (essentially) zero damping and a known modulus.

  2. Perform a simplex fit of the collected data to determine a set of dynamic model masses, spring constants, and damping coefficients that best match the collected damping and stiffness data.

  3. Calculate the SRSS of the discrepancies between the damping data points and the known zero damping of spring steel.

  4. Calculate the SRSS of the discrepancies between the stiffness data points and the known modulus of spring steel.

  5. Sum the two SRSS’s to get the dynamic calibration residual.

The residual value therefore indicates, in relative terms, how close the DMA is to behaving as an idealized three body system.
