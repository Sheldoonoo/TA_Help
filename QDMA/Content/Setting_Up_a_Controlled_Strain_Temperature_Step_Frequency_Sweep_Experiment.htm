Setting Up a Controlled Strain Temperature Step/Frequency Sweep Experiment

 

This experiment increases the temperature in steps and performs a frequency sweep at each step. This is the procedure used for frequency-based TTS experiments.

If you want to set up a DMA Temperature Step/Frequency Sweep procedure, you can choose one of two techniques—the DMA Wizard or the Experimental View - Procedure Page. See the instructions below for each technique.

When you perform a Multifrequency mode, DMA Temperature Step/Frequency Sweep experiment, the following parameters need to be chosen:

  • Select between Amplitude and Strain, see the guidelines below:

    • Amplitude is defined as the maximum amount of oscillatory motion in micrometers (one half peak-to-peak) in a DMA experiment.

    • Enter the desired oscillation amplitude for the sample. The DMA adjusts the oscillatory force applied to the sample to maintain this amplitude throughout the experiment.

NOTE:  In the multifrequency mode all experiments are performed at the same amplitude.

    • The range of acceptable oscillation amplitudes is ±0.5 to 10,000 micrometers (µm).  The actual usable range depends on geometry (clamp type), sample stiffness, and frequency.  The DMA can generate from 0.0001 to 18 N of force and the force required to sustain the frequency and amplitude requested must be within that range.  To determine approximate best amplitude for a specific sample, set an initial amplitude of 10-20 µm and an initial frequency (single) of 1 Hz.  Monitor the drive force at the temperature of interest to ensure it falls within the range.  If not, adjust the amplitude or change clamp.  For most samples 10 to 50 µm amplitudes should yield good results.  Amplitudes less than 5 mm are not recommended.  However, for extremely stiff materials where the force limit is reached during oscillation even after trying different clamp arrangements, amplitudes as low as 1 µm should still provide useful results.

    • Strain:   Oscillation strain is the maximum displacement normalized by an initial or reference condition. Based on the kinematics of deformation (tension, bending, or shear) the strain has mathematical expressions. See Calculations Based on Clamp Type for these expressions depending on the clamp being used.

    • A strain that is within the linear viscoelastic range (LVR) of the material is preferable. A good starting value is 0.01%. A multistrain experiment may be performed to assess the LVR accurately.

NOTE:  As a force-controlling instrument, the DMA adjusts the oscillatory force applied to the sample to maintain the specified amplitude or strain.

NOTE:  In a multifrequency mode each experiment is performed at a constant amplitude/strain.

  • Preload Force: This is the force applied to the sample prior to initiating the experiment.  

    • Preload force is not available when you select a nontensioning clamp (i.e., single/dual cantilever, shear sandwich).  

    • Preload force is required with the tensioning clamps (i.e., 3-point bending, compression, and tension film/fiber) when running multifrequency and multistrain experiments in order to sustain oscillation (ensure that force is applied to the sample throughout the complete oscillation).  Typical preload forces for those experiments are 0.01 to 0.5 N, depending on the stiffness of the sample.

  • Force Track:  Force track automatically adjusts the total force (preload and dynamic) applied to the sample during multifrequency and multistrain experiments.  

    • Force track is only used when one of the tensioning clamps (i.e., 3-point bend, compression, tension film and tension fiber) is selected. It is not needed for nontensioning clamps (i.e., single/dual cantilever, shear sandwich).

    • Force track essentially determines the dynamic force required to oscillate at the desired amplitude and frequency; it applies an additional static force that is a constant multiple of that dynamic force. This is necessary to avoid sample buckling in tension clamps and loss of contact with the sample for compression and 3-point bending clamps.

    • Recommended force track values are 120% for thin films and low denier fibers (tension film and tension fiber clamps), 150% for thermoplastics (3-point bending clamps), and 125% for general applications.

    • As the temperature increases, the modulus of the material drops (provided that there is no cross-linking reaction). Since the static force is adjusted in proportion to the dynamic force, the sample does not creep or undergo permanent deformation during the experiment.

  • Start & Final Temperature:  The temperature range of the DMA is -145 to 600°C.  This entire range is available if a Gas Cooling Accessory (GCA) is used.  Isothermal experiments can be run at any temperature within this range.  The initial and final temperature, as well as the temperature increment, are dependent on the transition(s) being evaluated.  Obviously, the initial and final temperatures should bracket the entire range of interest.  

  • Temperature Increment:  The temperature increment should be small enough to provide good resolution of events.  Typically, 3 to 10°C increment is used for TTS. If a two-decade wide frequency is selected, then the increment can be as high as 10°C.

  • Isothermal Soak Time:  Because of the mass of the DMA furnace and clamps, as well as the large size for most DMA samples, an "Isothermal soak time" (i.e., time to equilibrate at temperature) is recommended particularly when starting experiments at subambient temperatures.  Larger samples require longer soak times. For films, 3 to 5 minutes is usually sufficient.

  • Frequency Table:  All Multifrequency mode tests require that you select one or more frequencies. The frequency range for the DMA is 0.01 to 200Hz. "Logarithmically spaced" frequencies (e.g., 10, 5, 2, 1) are preferable rather than "linearly spaced" frequencies (e.g., 9, 7, 5, 3, 1). For more information on selecting frequencies, click here:  Frequency Table.

  • Additional Conditions:  There are additional experimental conditions that can be changed to optimize specific situations.  These are accessed using Advanced Parameters and Post-Test Conditions.  For most experiments, the system default values shown are recommended.

  • See also:  Guidelines for Running Subambient Experiments and Displaying the DMA Waveform.

Using the DMA Wizard :

Using the Experimental View Procedure Page:

  1. Display the Procedure Page. Select the desired experiment from the list of available preprogrammed templates.

  2. Enter the desired parameters. When you have finished setting up this procedure, you can enter more information by selecting the Notes Page and Summary Page for this run.

  3. Click the Apply button when finished to save these changes or the Append button to save the changes to this run and add another run to the end of the sequence.

NOTE:  You can use any test template as the basis for a custom test by selecting the desired test template from the list of tests, then click the Apply button and change the test to "Custom." Then you can click on the Procedure Page and then the Editor button to make the changes desired.
