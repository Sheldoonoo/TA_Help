Click the button to use the On-line Measuring System Selection Guide.

 

When selecting a measuring geometry it is important that you assess such factors as:

  •    What type of experiment do you wish to carry out?
  •    What is the sample behavior like?
  •    Does the sample contain particles?
  •    What is the real-life situation are you trying to recreate?

Sometimes the answers to all of the above questions are not known, but there are some basic ‘rules’ that will help you. However, it is also important to remember that you are measuring the bulk properties of the material itself and this should be independent of the type of geometry used (within reason!).

Cone and Plate/Parallel Plate Systems

The cone and plate and parallel plate systems have the advantage of only needing small sample volumes, they are easy to clean, they have low inertia and they can potentially achieve high shear rates. The additional advantage of using a cone and plate is that the shear rate is uniform throughout the sample. The advantage of the parallel plate is that it can accommodate large particles.

Generally, the cone and plate or parallel plate systems can be used for almost any sample. Because they are easier to set up and use, one of these systems would normally be the best choice and will provide optimum results.

They are both available in different sizes, therefore, it is important for you to understand how to choose the system with the correct dimensions.

Angles

Cones are supplied by TA Instruments in any angle up to 4°, usually in 0.5° increments. The 4° is the largest available, as the sample velocity profile becomes unpredictable at higher angles and the mathematical expression of a~ tan a is no longer valid.

The 4° cone is ideal for creep measurements because its large angle gives relatively small strains for a unit applied stress with respect to small angles.

The smaller the angle (or gap in a parallel plate system), the higher the maximum shear rate obtainable.

The main disadvantage with small angled cones is that the gap to be set is correspondingly small, hence, it is more important that the gap is set accurately.

Diameters

The smaller the diameter of a cone or parallel plate system, the larger the shear stress factor. This means that a small (e.g., 20 mm) diameter geometry should be used with stiffer materials or medium to high viscosity.

A 40 mm geometry is the most versatile and it usually allows the majority of medium viscosity materials to be measured.

A large diameter geometry (e.g., 60 mm) is most sensitive to stress changes and is used to measure low viscosity samples.

You need to load the sample correctly and be careful not to under or overfill the geometry. If this occurs, it would effectively change the diameters of the cones and, hence, adversely affect the shear stress factors.

Material

Stainless steel is relatively heavy, has a low coefficient of thermal expansion, is compatible with most samples, and is very robust.

Aluminium geometries are lighter than steel, but have a larger coefficient of thermal expansion. They will go to temperatures greater than 40°C, but are still heavier than acrylic.

Acrylic geometries are very light and are, therefore, most suitable to use with low viscosity samples. However, you should not use acrylic geometries above 40°C.

Preventing Solvent Evaporation

Evaporation can cause problems during measurements for samples that contain volatile solvents or are water-based. TA Instruments has overcome this problem by use of a solvent trap cover that sits over the geometry (but does not contact it). Solvent trap type geometries have a well on top of the geometry into which a small amount of the relevant solvent is placed. The solvent trap cover has a lip that sits in the solvent, which allows the free space around the sample to become saturated with the solvent vapour, preventing evaporation.

Preventing Slippage at Sample/Geometry Interface

Some samples, such as hydrogels, contain a lot of water that can migrate to the surface of the sample. A film layer can then form between the bulk of the material and the geometry surface. This will cause slippage at this interface. The problem can be alleviated by using special cross-hatched geometries, which, in effect, have the measuring surface slightly roughened. There is however a trade-off between absolute accuracy and repeatability.

Selecting the Measuring Geometry in the Software

Follow these steps to select the desired measuring geometry:

  1. Select Geometry from the menu.
  2. Click on Open.
  3. Select the required geometry file from the list.
  4. Press the Open button.
