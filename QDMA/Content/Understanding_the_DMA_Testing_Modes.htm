Understanding the DMA Testing Modes and Their Applications

 

The viscoelastic properties of a material can be characterized on the DMA using several experimental testing modes.  The next sections discuss these modes:

  • Dynamic Mechanical Analysis (DMA) Testing (also known as oscillatory measurements)

  • Multistrain Mode

  • Multistress Mode

  • Multifrequency Controlled Strain Mode

  • Multifrequency Controlled Stress Mode

  • Creep (or Step Stress) Testing

  • Stress Relaxation (or Step Strain) Testing

  • Controlled Force/Rate Testing

  • Isostrain Mode

  • Strain Rate Mode

Dynamic Mechanical Analysis Testing

Dynamic or oscillation experiments can be performed to characterize many properties of materials.  The following is a partial list.

  • Glass-transition temperature, Tg, and other secondary transitions - b, g, d (Single frequency Temperature Ramp)

  • Viscoelastic Response and Spectrum (Single Temperature Frequency Sweeps and/or Time-Temperature Superposition)

  • Linear Viscoelastic Region (Oscillatory Stress or Strain Sweep)

  • Damping characteristics (tan d)

  • Stress fatigue

  • Structure-related properties, especially with regard to:

    • Crosslinking, cure state

    • Crystallinity

    • Molecular Weight

    • Additives

    • Plasticizers

    • Blending

    • Aging

    • Orientation

There are several kinds of oscillation experiments that can be performed using the Q800. The basic parameters involved in these experiments are:

  1. Frequency

  2. Amplitude (or Strain)

  3. Drive Force (or Stress)

There are different modes that exist in the Thermal Advantage software that are derived by choosing what parameter to vary while keeping the others constant.

Multistrain Mode

In this mode, the frequency is held constant and the amplitude or strain is changed.  This is performed isothermally.  This mode is most beneficial in exploring the linear viscoelastic region (LVR) or materials.  The strain may be held constant too, and the properties of the material can be observed over a duration of time (strain fatigue).

Multistress Mode

The multistress mode is used to hold the frequency of oscillations constant, but change the peak stress being applied in the oscillations. The stress can be fixed as well to look at stress fatigue over a period of time.

Multifrequency Controlled Strain Mode

In this mode, the frequency is changeable, while the amplitude (or strain) of oscillation is held constant.  A frequency sweep may be performed to evaluate the viscoelastic response of a material by the observed changes in the storage and loss moduli and tan d.  Determination of the Tg and other transitions of materials as well as other structure-property relationships may be performed using a temperature ramp or hold while performing a frequency sweep (single or multiple frequencies).  This mode enables the user to perform Time-Temperature Superposition in the frequency domain.

Creep (or Step Stress) Testing

In a creep test, a finite stress is instantaneously applied to the sample and held constant for a period of time.  At the end of that period, the stress is removed instantaneously.  The resulting changes in strain are measured as a function of time.  The material properties obtained from this experiment are:

  • The creep compliance [J(t)] is calculated by dividing the time-dependent strain g(t) by the applied stress t.  Once the creep compliance has reached a steady state (constant slope with time), the sample recovery can be measured by instantaneously removing the stress (stress = 0), and monitoring the recovered strain as a function of time.  

  • The recoverable compliance, Jr, can be calculated by dividing the time-dependent recoverable strain gr(t) by the stress applied in the creep zone.  Sometimes, the ultimate value of strain achieved in the creep step, gu, is incorporated, such that the Jr = (gu â€“ gr)/s. The recoverable compliance will eventually stabilize to a constant value.  If the response is linear, the stabilized value of compliance is referred to as the equilibrium recoverable compliance, Je0.  

Creep/recovery behavior is shown graphically in the figure below. Viscoelastic behavior in creep can be represented by the Kelvin or Voigt model. This model consists of a spring and a dashpot combined in parallel. Upon the application of stress on the system, the dashpot retards the elastic growth of strain in the spring with time, until the spring has reached a maximum extension. When the stress is removed, there is no instantaneous elastic recovery because the dashpot again retards the recovery until the initial unstretched state is reached. The time constant (tc) is known as the retardation time and it represents the characteristic time dependence of the system.

Creep experiments help to determine the behavior of materials under different stresses and their elasticity (or lack thereof) when the stress is removed (See figure below).  Using creep TTS, one can look at long-time effects of materials under stress.

Stress Relaxation (or Step Strain) Testing

In a stress relaxation test, a strain is instantaneously applied to the sample, and the stress required to maintain that strain is measured as a function of time.  The stress relaxation modulus [G(t) using shear sandwich clamps or E(t) using tension, compression, or bending clamps] is calculated as the time-dependent stress divided by the constant strain.  The sample recovery can also be monitored with time upon release of the strain. Stress relaxation behavior is shown graphically in the figure below.

Viscoelastic behavior in stress relaxation can be represented by the Maxwell model.  This model consists of a spring and a dashpot combined in series.  When the strain is applied, the spring undergoes a step change in strain because the dashpot cannot respond instantaneously to the step change.  With time, the dashpot does relieve the stress thus generated by moving to unstretch the spring.

If you know that a certain material undergoes a definite amount of deformation in terms of strain, you could mimic that using a stress-relaxation experiment by applying the relevant strain and observing the response over a period of time.  In conjunction with the TTS capability, you could evaluate what would happen to a rubber-washer, for example, that is pressed by a certain strain over a long period of time but within an experimentally feasible duration.

Controlled Force and Rate

With these modes, one can control the force being applied to a sample directly, or affect the rate at which the sample may be deformed in terms of displacement or strain.  Provided the stiffness of the material is within the limits of the instrument, experiments can be performed to evaluate the conventional Modulus of Elasticity of materials (ASTM D-5934, D-638, D-882).  Essentially, the stress is plotted versus strain, and the slope of the linear region is evaluated to obtain the Modulus of Elasticity, as shown below.

The Controlled Force mode also lets you apply a constant force onto a sample while ramping the temperature to see changes in dimension, especially during curing or for heat-shrink applications.  This method can also be used with 3-point bending clamps to evaluate the Deflection Temperature Under Load (DTUL), commonly referred to as the Heat Deflection Temperature (HDT).

Isostrain Mode

The Isostrain mode can be used in conjunction with film tension clamps only.  This is used to apply a constant strain on a piece of film and perform a temperature ramp.  Essentially, it is a combination of a stress-relaxation experiment and temperature ramp.  This allows the user to evaluate properties like shrinkage force.

Strain Rate Mode

The Strain Rate mode can be used to collect stress vs. strain data equivalent to that obtained from a universal testing machine.  In this mode a sample is stretched or compressed at a specified strain or displacement rate until one of the following end-conditions is met:

  • The sample breaks or yields.

  • The DMA 18N static force limit is reached.

  • The specified final displacement or final strain value is attained.
