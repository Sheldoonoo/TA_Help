Introduction to Creep Tests

 

Many important processes are driven by very low stresses, such as those produced by gravity or surface tension. These include settling and creaming in dispersions, levelling after application in surface coatings, and slumping and sagging in paints. Moreover the handling properties of some materials are affected by elasticity; the ability to recover in some part their original shape after forced deformation.

These properties can be investigated using a creep test, in which a very low shearing stress (force acting over an area), s, is applied to the sample, and the resulting strain (displacement), g, is monitored. It is usual to plot the compliance, J, defined as the strain divided by the stress, against time. If a low stress is placed on a solid sample, it will respond by deforming almost instantaneously to a new position, and then stopping. When the stress is removed, the sample will immediately recover its original dimensions. The compliance will depend on the material: the stiffer the material, the lower the compliance. Typical values are about 10-3 / Pa for a 2% gelatin solution in water, 5 x 10-5 / Pa for foam rubber, 3 x 10-7 / Pa for natural rubber, and 2 x 10-9 / Pa for nylon. Figure 1 shows creep behavior for an ideal solid with a compliance of 10-3 / Pa, the stress being applied at time zero and removed after 1000 sec.

 

Figure 1: Creep curve for an ideal solid with a compliance of 10-3 Pa-1, with stress applied for 1000 seconds, then removed.

 

If the stress is placed on a liquid material, it will deform continuously, the rate of deformation being inversely proportional to the viscosity of the liquid. When the stress is removed the sample will cease to deform, and will not show any recovery. Typical values of viscosity are 1 mPa s (milliPascal second) for water, 1 Pa s for glycerine, and about 1000 Pa for polymer melts. Figure 2 shows a creep curve for an ideal liquid of viscosity 1 Pa s.

 

Figure 2: Creep curve for an ideal liquid of viscosity 1 Pa s, with stress applied for 1000 seconds, then removed

 

Many industrial materials are neither completely solid nor completely liquid. They show properties that are some way between these limiting forms of behavior, and are called viscoelastic. When a stress is placed on a viscoelastic material, the deformation will be retarded. When the stress is removed, the recovery will also be retarded (Figure 3).

 

Figure 3: Creep curve for a viscoelastic material showing retarded deformation, with stress applied for 1000 seconds then removed.

 

In some cases, a sample will show both retarded deformation and continuous flow, in which case recovery after removal of the stress will be incomplete (Figure 4).

Figure 4: Creep curve for a viscoelastic material showing continuous flow, with stress applied for 1000 seconds, then removed.

 

The elastic properties of the material are then available from the retarded deformation and the recovery parts of the curve, while the continuous flow part provides the viscosity at the applied stress. This is identical to the low shear viscosity obtained from a flow curve.

Most industrial dispersions do not fulfill the criteria for this expression, but the viscosity will nonetheless give a qualitative indication of the relative stability to sedimentation of comparable dispersions. In some cases the stability against particle aggregation or emulsion coalescence can also be predicted from the low shear viscosity. Food “stabilisers”, for example, do not impart stability in the thermodynamic sense, but reduce the rate of particle motion by increasing the viscosity of the medium. Similar expressions to Equation 1 exist to predict levelling after application in surface coatings, and sagging or slumping in paint films.

 

It is also important that industrial and biological materials should have the correct degree of elasticity. A printing ink, for example, will fail to enter the nip if it is too elastic, but will give poor dot definition if it insufficiently so. Contact adhesives are lightly cross-linked, and therefore elastic, to prevent excessive surface wetting. The texture and feel of foodstuffs is in part due to elasticity; butter has relatively low elasticity, jam is highly elastic. Some industrial materials are formulated to be viscoelastic to prevent dripping. After pouring, the ruptured stream will recoil rather than drip.
