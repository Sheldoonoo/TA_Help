Introducing the TGA Q500 & Q50

The TGA Q Series™ instruments (TGA Q500 with Autosampler shown to the left and TGA Q50 with EGA furnace shown to the right) operate on a null balance principle. Physically attached to a taut-band meter movement, the balance arm is maintained in a horizontal reference position by an optically actuated servo loop. When the balance is in a null position, a flag located on top of the balance arm blocks an equal amount of light to each of the photodiodes (the light is supplied by a constant current infrared LED). As sample weight is lost or gained, the beam becomes unbalanced, causing an unequal amount of light to strike the photodiodes. The unbalance signal, call the error signal, is acted upon the control circuitry and reduced to zero, or nulled. This is accomplished by an increase or decrease in the current to the meter movement, causing it to rotate back to its original position (null position). The change in current necessary to accomplish this task is directly proportional to the change in mass of the sample. This current is converted to the weight signal.

NOTE:  Previous models of the TGA Q Series instruments have a tare tube that is removed to access the tare side pan. See Installing/Replacing and Aligning Hang-Down Wires for more information.

TGA Weight Ranges

The TGA has two weight ranges: 1 gram and 200 milligrams. Both ranges are continuous over their weight loss operating range, which means that the entire weight loss range can be viewed without any loss of information. The weight loss operating ranges are:

  • 1 gm to 0.1 µg for the 1 gm range.

  • 200 mg to 0.1 µg for the 200 mg range.

Negative weight (tare imbalance) is limited to 150 mg in the 1 gm range, and to 15 mg in the 200 mg range. Range control is set on the Tools/Instrument Preferences/TGA Page.

During normal operation of the TGA, the sample may evolve gases. To prevent back diffusion of these liberated gases to the balance chamber, the balance chamber is purged with an inert gas (standard furnace = 40 mL/min, EGA furnace = 10 mL/min). An inert gas must be used to prevent contamination or corrosion of the balance. See also:  Recommended Purge Gases and Flow Rates

Heating rate and sample temperature are measured by the thermocouple located above the sample. This enables the controller to program and maintain the sample environment at your selected rate.

TGA Theory of Operation

Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) is a thermal analysis technique for measuring the amount and rate of change in sample mass as a function of temperature and time. It is used to characterize any material that exhibits weight loss or phase changes as a result of decomposition, dehydration, and oxidation. Two modes are commonly used for investigating thermal stability behavior in controlled atmosphere: (1) dynamic, in which the temperature is increased at a linear rate, and (2) isothermal, in which the temperature is kept constant.

TGA Balance

The TGA balance assembly consists of the balance meter movement, the balance arm, the balance arm sensor, the hang-down wire assemblies, the sample pan, and the tare pan as seen in the figure below.

Back to top

The balance meter movement is a taut-band meter movement that attaches to the balance arm.

The balance arm is a rhombic piece of aluminum attached to the meter movement. It is in a null balance system. A hang-down loop is attached to each end to hold the hang-down wires.

The balance arm sensor is a printed circuit board assembly that detects the null position of the meter movement. The balance beam sensor is mounted above the balance arm. It is used in conjunction with the analog circuitry to maintain a null position.

The TGA has two hang-down wire assemblies—one for the tare pan and one for the sample pan. Each assembly consists of a hang-down wire and loop. The hang-down wire has hooks at each end and connects the pan to the loop. The loop is used to connect the hang-down wire to the balance arm. The longer hang-down wire (4 inches) is for the sample pan.

Sample pans are available in the following materials and sizes (all pans are 0.4 inches in diameter):

  • Platinum 50 and 100µL

  • Alumina ceramic 100, 250, and 500µL

  • Aluminum 100µL

The tare pan holds the counter-balance weight that mechanically subtracts out the weight of the sample pan.
