Understanding the Air Bearing

 

As its name suggests, an air bearing is one that utilizes air as the lubricating medium. This allows virtually friction-free application of torque. The design of an air bearing tends to be a compromise between several characteristics such as air consumption, friction, stiffness and tolerance to contamination and misuse.

The amount of air consumed is related to the pressurized bearing clearance. To minimize air consumption, a small clearance (<10mm) is needed. However, as air has a finite viscosity (0.0018 mPa.s), small gaps will give rise to high shear rates and correspondingly the friction will increase.

If large gaps are used, the shear rate is lowered and friction is reduced, but the stiffness of the air bearing is also reduced. Thus, a compromise in the design of an air bearing is needed for optimal performance.

The air bearing used in the AR1000/2000 rheometers uses a mixture of proven bearing techniques with novel materials. The surfaces can be easily machined to tolerances of less than 1mm, providing an extremely smooth finish.

The bearing is designed to be virtually friction- free, so that it moves under the smallest of forces. Even turbulent flow in the assembly is sufficient to make the bearing rotate. This known as the windmill effect. To ensure that the bearing is steady before measurement, this effect is countered by the application of a very small torques in the opposite direction to that of the movement. This process is called Rotational Mapping and a calibration procedure can be carried out periodically.
